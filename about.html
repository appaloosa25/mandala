The publication

About Mandala

An independent journal committed to research and analysis that takes cities seriously — as political objects, economic systems, and lived environments.

What we publish

Mandala publishes original research, policy analysis, and field-based reporting on urban planning and the built environment. Our focus is South Asian cities — though we regularly draw on comparative evidence from cities across Southeast Asia, Africa, and Latin America where the parallels are instructive.

We are interested in questions that sit at the intersection of spatial planning, political economy, and lived experience: Who decides how cities grow? Who benefits, and who is displaced? What makes planning interventions succeed or fail in practice?

"The city is not a text to be read but a system to be inhabited. Urban planning research must take both dimensions seriously."

— Editorial statement, Mandala Vol. 1

Our editorial standards

All research articles submitted to Mandala undergo double-blind peer review by two external reviewers with relevant domain expertise. Policy essays and field reports are reviewed by at least one member of our editorial board before acceptance.

We prioritise work that is methodologically rigorous and written to be understood beyond the specialist reader. We will ask authors to revise work that is inaccessible not because it is complex, but because it has not been written clearly.

We do not publish work that has appeared in substantially the same form elsewhere. We do not charge article processing fees. All accepted work is published under a Creative Commons CC BY 4.0 licence — free to read, share, and build on with attribution.

The name

The mandala is one of the oldest frameworks in South Asian urbanism. In Kautilya's Arthashastra and Varahamihira's Brihat Samhita, it describes the concentric organisation of the ideal city — a spatial logic radiating outward from a symbolic centre. We chose the name not to invoke nostalgia for ancient forms, but because it captures something real about cities: they are systems of concentric relationships, of centre and periphery, core and margin, that cannot be understood from any single vantage point.

Good urban planning research moves between those rings — from the macro political economy to the neighbourhood street to the individual household — and takes each seriously.

Open access commitment

Mandala is fully open access. We believe that research on cities — particularly cities in the global South — should not be locked behind subscription paywalls accessible only to institutions in wealthy countries. Every article we publish is freely available to anyone with an internet connection, in perpetuity.

We are funded through a combination of institutional support from our founding partner organisations and small grants from urban research foundations. We do not accept advertising, and we do not accept payments for editorial consideration.

Advisory board

Mandala's editorial decisions are supported by an advisory board of senior researchers and practitioners drawn from urban planning, economics, law, and public health. Board members are drawn from institutions including IIT Delhi, TISS Mumbai, Azim Premji University, and the Indian Institute for Human Settlements.

Submit your research

We welcome submissions from researchers, planners, and practitioners. We particularly encourage work from early-career researchers and authors based in cities that are underrepresented in the urban studies literature.
